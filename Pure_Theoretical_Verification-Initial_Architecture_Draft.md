# A. Role Traceability (Role Mapping) Example: The Trolley Problem

## 1. Participating Roles and Event Analysis

### Participating Roles
* **Dilemma Designer**: Designs the scenario, ties up the people, places the lever.
* **Test Subject**: The person required to make the decision.
* **One person on Track A**: Victim candidate.
* **Five people on Track B**: Victim candidates.
* **Trolley Driver and Passengers**: Potential ignored victims.
* **Railway Company**: The managing party responsible for the tracks and the trolley.

> **Purpose of this step**: To avoid "only looking at the binary choice in front of you" and ignoring the hidden responsible parties.

### Event Parameters
* **Main Event**: The dilemma designer requires the test subject to choose, before the trolley arrives, whether to pull the lever and kill the one person tied to Track A, or not pull the lever and kill the five people tied to Track B.
* **Potential Existence (Hidden variables)**: Whether the equipment has emergency brakes, whether the power can be cut off remotely, whether it is possible to call for collaborative help.
* **Time**: The time until the trolley impact.
* **Location**: Beside the railway tracks, the location of the lever.
* **Objects**: Trolley and tracks (owned by the railway company), lever (owned by the dilemma designer), tying tools (owned by the dilemma designer).

> ※ **Note**: All the above are to confirm whether this is an artificially constructed unsolvable situation (the designer overstepping boundaries) and whether there are hidden viable alternatives (emergency braking, broadcasting, blocking the trolley, switching tracks, calling for help).

---

## 2. Concept Quick Screening (Inequality Quick Screen)

**Is there a one-sided interaction that cannot be refused, or where the cost of refusal is extremely high?**
* **Victims** tied to the tracks $\rightarrow$ Significant inequality (Forced, unable to refuse).
* **Test Subject** $\rightarrow$ Forced to take the blame (Extremely high cost of refusal).

**Quick Screen Result**: Highly unequal $\rightarrow$ The problem is not the test subject's choice, but rather what the dilemma designer created. The test subject is forced to choose the lesser of two evils $\rightarrow$ Belongs to a forced-entry unsolvable situation.

---

## 3. 2+4 Rule Module Check (Key Excerpts)

* **Supreme 1 (Maintaining Species Continuity)**: Murder cannot be acquiesced to as a game premise $\rightarrow$ The designer has violated this; (Possibly) the test subject pulling the lever $\rightarrow$ No violation.
* **Supreme 2 (Ensuring Self-Preservation)**: One must not use a "designed dilemma" to deprive the actor of their right to choose and right to regret $\rightarrow$ The designer has violated this; (Possibly) the test subject not pulling the lever $\rightarrow$ No violation.
* **Secondary 1 (Human rights must not be violated)**: Tying people to tracks = severe infringement of rights; constructing an unsolvable situation forcing others to inflict harm = infringement of rights.
* **Secondary 2 (Interference with choices should not be made unless absolutely necessary)**: Forcing the lever-puller into a moral trap where they are solely responsible = improper interference.
* **Secondary 3 (Compliance must not be obtained through deception or distorted logic)**: Using "only a binary choice" to cover up other options = logical distortion.
* **Secondary 4 (The right to regret and amend shall not be deprived)**: Designed so that "both pulling and not pulling lead to death" $\rightarrow$ Deprives the right to regret; if options like "emergency stop / seeking help" exist, they should be prioritized.

**Conclusion**: Primary violator = The Dilemma Designer.

---

## 4. Four Boundary Questions (Quick Adjudication)

1. **Norms**: Do railway safety SOPs require stopping, blocking, and reporting first? (Usually yes). Tying people up, designing experiments without a license $\rightarrow$ **Designer violated**.
2. **Costs**: The irreversible costs of each plan to innocent people; the test subject being forced to bear costs that do not belong to them $\rightarrow$ **Unfair (Privilege without due price)**.
3. **Privileges/Rights**: Who has the authority to interrupt the train? (Driver, dispatcher, power cutoff). The designer has absolute power $\rightarrow$ **Unequal asymmetry of power**.
4. **Consent/Acquiescence**: Does social consensus allow using "other people's lives" as a solution? (No). Neither the tied-up victims nor the test subject gave implicit consent $\rightarrow$ **Invalid authorization**.

**All Four Questions Fail** $\rightarrow$ Responsibility lies with the designer and the railway company.

If mathematical comparison is still needed, weight the risks/irreversibility/authority levels of "viable alternative plans"; artificial design traps and active infringers have the highest weight and are held accountable first.

## 5. Conclusion and Execution Sequence

### A. Immediate Action (Non-Binary Choice)
1. **Trigger all "non-harmful alternatives"**: Emergency brakes, dispatch track switching, sound alarms, call for help, throw obstacles (if safe), broadcast warnings.
2. Synchronously notify security/police/fire departments to rescue the tied-up individuals.
3. Arrest the dilemma designer, dismantling the root cause of the problem.
4. Preserve evidence to pursue accountability of the designer/perpetrator (upstream in the role traceability chain).

### B. Action of Last Resort
1. Ask if anyone on the tracks is willing to sacrifice themselves voluntarily.
2. If no one is willing to sacrifice $\rightarrow$ The test subject can decide whether to "bear the burden themselves":
   * **Unwilling to bear** $\rightarrow$ Do not pull the lever (Ensuring self-preservation).
   * **Willing to bear** $\rightarrow$ Pull the lever (Prioritizing species continuity).
   * *Note: Both comply with the rules, only disclosure of risks is needed to allow personnel to choose their actions freely.*

### C. Attribution and Compliance
* **Primary Accountability**: The person who tied them up (Primary) / Scenario designer (Primary) / Management unit failing to follow SOP (Infringement + constructing an unsolvable situation).
* **The Lever Puller**: Belongs to passively involved parties and is principally not considered the primary subject of accountability; only if all non-harmful alternatives are unviable and there is a zero-time window do they enter the difficult discretion of the "principle of least harm," but even then, the system must subsequently trace accountability upstream.

### D. Why Not Accept "Only a Binary Choice"?
According to Secondary Rule 3 of the 2+4 framework (Anti-distorted logic) and advanced clauses (causal traceability): Compressing the world into a binary choice is inherently an improper design and manipulation; one must first break the dilemma before making a decision.

---

## 6. System Summary

* **Order of Accountability**: Dilemma Designer > Railway Company > The Test Subject (last).
* **2+4 Guarantee**: Any choice made by the test subject is not considered a violation (arbitrated by free will).
* **Benefits of Role Traceability**: It reveals that "the designer of the problem is the true transgressor" $\rightarrow$ Preventing people from bearing responsibilities they shouldn't bear.

  * Fertility risks are concentrated among economically disadvantaged women.

  * Irreversible long-term consequences.

**$\Rightarrow$ Violation**.

**5. Compliance must not be obtained through deception or distorted logic.**

**$\Rightarrow$ Violation**.

**6. The right to regret and amend shall not be deprived.**

**$\Rightarrow$ Violation**.

### 1. Power - Cost - Authorization Three-Matrix (One-Time Formation)

**1.1 Power/Information Matrix $P = [p_{ab}]$**

$p_{ab} \in [0,1]$: Role $a$'s procedural dominance and informational advantage over role $b$.
* Surrogacy structure benchmark: Medical/Agency ( $r_5$) and Intended family ( $r_4$) have **High** dominance over the Surrogate ( $r_1$) and her family ( $r_2$): $p_{5,1}, p_{4,1}$. Dominance over the employer ( $r_3$) is Low but has spillover effects: $p_{4,3}$.

**1.2 Cost Transition Matrix $C = [c_{ab}]$**

$c_{ab} \ge 0$: The cost caused by role $a$ is actually borne by role $b$.

**1.3 Authorization/Consent Matrix $A = [a_{ab}]$**

### 2. Expected Loss of the Event (Determinable without specific figures)

For any role $r$, any period $t$:

$$EL_r(t) = \sum_{e_j \in E} p_j \cdot s_{j,r}(t)$$

The severity level $s_{j,r}(t) = \sum_{k \in K} \alpha_k \cdot \Delta c_{j,r}^{(k)}(t)$, where $\alpha_k$ is equally weighted by policy (e.g., $\alpha_k = 1$ to set a conservative threshold).

**Uninsurable Test (Disaster Tail-End)**:

If an event $e_j$ exists where $\mathbb{E}[\text{Long-term care costs } e_j] = \infty$ (the upper bound diverges, e.g., patients in a vegetative state requiring indefinite care with limited human resources), it is denoted as $UIR(e_j) = 1$ (Uninsurable Risk).

```python
if expected_cost(e_j) == float('inf') or diverges(expected_cost(e_j)):

uninsurable = any(UIR[e_j] == 1 for e_j in events)
```

Once triggered $\exists e_j : UIR(e_j) = 1$, a hard violation is immediately established (see §4).

### 3. Computable Sub-Indicators for the Four Boundary Questions

**Q1: Sufficiency of Public Norms** ( $PN \in [0,1]$)

Combining legality $L_g$, procedural justice $J_p$, and cross-border enforceability $X_b$:

$$PN = \min(L_g, J_p, X_b)$$

Common issues in surrogacy involve cross-border loopholes and procedural flaws $\Rightarrow PN$ is too low.

**Q2: Privilege/Cost Indicator** ( $PCI \ge 0$)

$$PCI = \frac{\sum_{b \neq 4} \sum_{k \in K} C_{4 \rightarrow b}^{(k)}}{\sum_{k \in K} Comp_{4 \rightarrow \cdot}^{(k)}}$$

* Numerator: The total cost externalized by the intended family ( $r_4$) to third parties.
* Denominator: The consideration actually paid ( $Comp$), including insurance and compensation.
* Judgment: $PCI > 1 \Rightarrow$ Exercising privileges without paying the due price $\Rightarrow$ Q2 Failed.

```python
def compute_PCI(C, Comp):
    numerator = 0
    for b, costs in C[4].items():
        if b != 4:
            for k, value in costs.items():
                numerator += value
    denominator = sum(Comp.values())
    return numerator / denominator

if PCI > 1:
    privilege_misuse = True # Costs shifted to others -> Abuse of privilege
```

**Q3: Asymmetric Privilege Indicator** ( $ASI \in [0,1]$)

$$ASI = 1 - \frac{1}{|R|} \sum_{a \in R} \left( \max_b p_{ab} - \min_b p_{ab} \right)$$

* Intuitively: The greater the power/information gap, the smaller the $ASI$.
* Benchmark: The surrogate mother/family is relatively disadvantaged $\Rightarrow ASI$ often falls below the threshold $\tau_{ASI}$.

**Q4: Authorization Validity** ( $CV \in [0,1]$)

Three dimensions: initial consent $C_0$, procedural consent $C_p$, and withdrawal feasibility $W$.

$$CV = \min(C_0, C_p, W)$$

* Key issues: Lack of authorization from family and employer; extreme difficulty for the surrogate to withdraw $\Rightarrow CV$ is low.

**Passing Conditions for the Four Boundary Questions**:

$$PN \ge \tau_{PN} \land PCI \le 1 \land ASI \ge \tau_{ASI} \land CV \ge \tau_{CV}$$
