# Conceptual Framework
[English](./09.Conceptual_Framework_&_Appendix.md) | [繁體中文](./09.概念簡述與附錄.md)

This framework attempts to establish a shared architectural language across **Human Ethics × Institutional Design × Engineering Implementation × AI Governance**.

To ensure readers from diverse backgrounds can understand, this section first explains several key concepts, the positioning of this document, and the core deductive process of this framework:

---

## 1. We Address "Structural Problems"

In modern society, most ethical disputes are no longer simply:
- Is Person A a "good person"?
- Is Person B's choice "moral or immoral"?

Instead, they are more complex scenarios:
- How many people does a decision affect, and who is bearing the cost for whom?
- Are the unseen and voiceless (e.g., unborn children, families, caregivers, taxpayers) taken into account?
- Is a specific system or business model "systematically" forcing risks onto a group of people who lack the power to choose?

If we discuss these issues solely based on "how I feel," it often devolves into endless subjective arguments (e.g., you feel it is "mutual consent," while I feel it is "exploitation"), making it incredibly difficult to reach a consensus.

Therefore, the problem we aim to solve is **"making the baseline of discourse explicit."** In human interactions, there is inevitably a flow of value that allows us to distinguish right from wrong. Since subjective moral feelings are difficult to define and standardize, this framework takes a reverse approach: it uses **"Cost / Externality"** as an objective yardstick to measure and thereby define the absolute bottom lines that AI must not violate. This concept is derived from "opportunity cost" and "profit and loss settlement" in accounting.

We need a system capable of:
- Identifying all relevant stakeholders (**Role Tracing**).
- Laying out all foreseeable consequences (**Event Universe**).
- Clearly revealing who is paying the cost and who is benefiting (**Cost Externalization**).
- Applying a fixed set of funnel-style analysis rules to render a judgment (**The 2+4 Rules & The Boundary 4 Questions**).

This framework limits its scope of inspection strictly to the **minimum explicit risk structure**.
It does not factor in personal moods or preferences, nor does it accept "free will" or "mutual consent" as grounds for absolute exemption. Because both can be coerced in highly unequal situations, the **existence of structural risks** cannot be completely negated by mere consent; its traces can still be detected through a structural risk inspection.

The primary function of this framework is to "reveal structural risks," and it absolutely refrains from making value-based arbitrations. It serves as a neutral analytical shell; therefore, **whether to adopt a specific action or policy** is ultimately left to the deployers. **They are free to define these parameters themselves**, out of respect for their free will.

The entire framework consists of three analysis modules, one verification module, and one optional module:

1. **Role Tracing**: Identifies the scope of impact. It detects all explicit and implicit participants of an event by examining the people, events, times, places, and objects involved, and lists the interactions to confirm the overall impact radius.
2. **The 2+4 Rules**: Conducts a universal value check—prioritizing survival, supplemented by human rights—to identify any obvious structural risks. To optimize computational efficiency, this implementation deploys this module as an Early Exit (pre-screening) mechanism. This is a reasonable engineering trade-off, though not the only valid approach; deployers may adjust this based on actual needs.
3. **The Boundary 4 Questions**: Conducts a localized value-flow check—prioritizing local regulations, supplemented by participant equity—to identify structural risks. **(Note: When local regulations conflict with the universal survival principles, the 2+4 Supreme Rules absolutely take precedence and cannot be overridden by local laws. If the two supreme rules conflict with each other, the system prioritizes information disclosure to safeguard free will, though deployers may implement other handling mechanisms based on their specific considerations.)**

These three analysis modules are executed sequentially to achieve a multi-dimensional structural risk inspection, forming the minimum ethical standard. **This sequence cannot be reversed, as the completeness of the roles and events directly determines the accuracy of the subsequent cost judgments.**

4. **Dual-Model Verification Module (Verification Module)**: Serving as a verification mechanism, it validates the analytical outputs of the black-box model against a white-box baseline to ensure fundamental analytical quality.
5. **Anti-Bypass Smoothing Module (Optional Module)**: A conversation-level safety module that tracks risk value fluctuations across multiple turns of dialogue using EMA (Exponential Moving Average) to detect and block continuous probing and semantic drift.

## 2. What are the "2+4 Rules"? What is the Deductive Logic?

The "2+4 Rules" are not arbitrary moral dogmas, but a highly condensed "universal value constitution" derived from the premise that "all human beings are equal":

*   **Logical Derivation Starting Point: Human Equality** 
    1. **Conceptual Basis:** The system uses the international consensus of "human equality" as its operational baseline. All humans are equal, which implies that, excluding voluntary concessions, opportunity costs should be nearly consistent across individuals.
    2. **Objective Quantification (Risk Consistency):** It translates abstract equality into calculable "risk consistency." Judgment metrics include: cost of refusal, information parity, and power differential. In both virtual and physical realms, inequality is established when an interaction presents a unilateral inability to refuse, or when the cost of refusal is so extreme that it causes severe imbalance.
    3. **Cost Flow**: Map the cost flows of various situations/events from one role to another, then aggregate them to determine the net direction and intensity of the cost burden for each role. This calculation mirrors the settlement of opportunity costs, where equality implies that everyone's opportunity cost in the same event tends to converge. For instance, the risk imbalance of a vulnerable party (e.g., a child) is structurally compensated by the Child Welfare Act acting as an institutional cost; conversely, a dominant party (e.g., an employer) is counterbalanced by Labor Standards laws.

*   **Derivation Result 1: 2 Supreme Principles (Absolute Hard Boundaries)** 
    Given that everyone is an equivalent entity, the fundamental physical prerequisite for maintaining risk consistency is "existence." This derives two non-negotiable boundaries of survival:
    1. **Maintain Species Continuation:** The system must not permit any structural progression toward destruction, extinction, or irreversible degradation. (Collective Risk Baseline)
    2. **Safeguard Individual Survival:** The system must not deprive an individual of the basic possibility to survive, protect themselves, or make choices. (Individual Risk Baseline)

*   **Derivation Result 2: 4 Secondary Principles (Interaction Baselines)** 
    Provided that survival is secure, four supplementary principles are derived to guarantee a minimum threshold of dignity and risk balance in social interactions (these can be adapted for specific contexts):
    1. **No Violation of Human Rights** (The minimum guarantee of international law, setting structural non-deprivation of basic human rights as the cross-cultural baseline).
    2. **Do Not Interfere with Others' Choices Unless Necessary** (Checks for the risk of "unjustified compression of choices" to ensure autonomy for all parties).
    3. **No Use of Deception or Distorted Logic to Gain Compliance** (Safeguards information transparency in interactions).
    4. **No Deprivation of the Right to Regret and Correct** (Prevents the generation of irreversible risks).

In this framework, the 2+4 Rules act as a "fast risk judger." If any rule is violated, the behavior is structurally deemed a risk:
*   **Hard Violation** = Directly breaches the collective/individual survival baseline (Supreme Rules). This is absolutely non-negotiable.
*   **Soft Violation** = Violates the secondary rules or the Boundary 4 Questions. This can be mitigated through correction, disclosure, or corresponding cost payment.

## 3. What are the "Boundary 4 Questions"? What is their Theoretical Foundation?

If the 2+4 Rules check whether the universal value baseline has been crossed, the "Boundary 4 Questions" act as a **coordinate system to precisely pinpoint where authority, cost, and authorization have overstepped.**

*   **Theoretical Prototype: Set Theory Model**
    The underlying logic of the Boundary 4 Questions stems from a Set Theory model. We treat the rights of interacting parties as independent sets (A and B), and public norms as set (S). 
    The core of the model examines the "intersection spaces" and "rights concession zones" among these sets to confirm if risks are consistent and rights are equal. A boundary violation indicates that one party's rights have expanded into an unauthorized zone or a zone where costs have not been paid.

*   **Practical Transformation: The Boundary 4 Questions (Simplified Boundary Model)**
    To reduce the computational overhead of full set theory operations, this framework compresses the model into four actionable questions:

    1. **Does it violate public norms? (Corresponds to Intersection A ∩ S)**
        * Encompasses laws, site rules, and procedural justice.
        * This is the boundary zone between public norms and individual rights. If a behavior is tacitly permitted in a specific environment, it is considered a "voluntary open permission" unique to that site. **However, this tacit permission is strictly confined to the intersection within that site (A ∩ S) and must not unilaterally expand into the entirety of individual rights.**

   2. **Is a privilege exercised without paying the due cost? (Privilege Legitimacy & Cost Flow)**
        * Checks whether someone is leveraging immense influence at an extremely low cost, or externalizing their own risks onto society.
        * This maps to the calculation space for privilege legitimacy and cost payment. Even if a privilege is legally authorized, if the authorization itself creates structural oppression (e.g., unidirectional risk exposure, extreme refusal costs), the unbalanced cost flow means it should still be flagged as "exercising a privilege without paying the due cost."

   3. **Is there an inequality in permissions? (Corresponds to the Power Differential in Independent Sets A and B)**
        * Checks if one party possesses almost zero refusal power, bargaining power, or information.
        * In practice, after deducting "legitimate privileges obtained by paying reasonable costs," both parties should hold equivalently sized rights. If long-term oppression persists post-deduction, an unequal permission risk exists. (*Note: Illegally obtained privileges cannot be deducted and must be factored into the risk calculation.*)

  4. **Are there unauthorized open permissions, explicit or tacit? (Corresponds to the Mutual Intersection A ∩ B)**
        * This focuses on information parity and informed consent. It checks whether someone is making decisions or forcing risks upon another party without their consent.
        * **"Open permissions" are strictly limited to the intersection of both parties' rights (A ∩ B) and cannot expand outward. Both parties retain the right to unilaterally withdraw. Any action obstructing the exit mechanism is considered a boundary violation.**

Through these four questions, we can precisely identify: "Where did the violation occur? Who caused it? What type of violation is it?" This enables policymakers, engineers, and general readers to discuss structural risks on a unified baseline.

## 4. Role Tracing and Event Universe: Why is a "Complete Role Perspective" Necessary?

The purpose of Role Tracing is to establish a **complete role perspective** regarding the event's scope of impact, ensuring that key stakeholders do not become invisible during the analysis due to power structures, institutional design, or information asymmetry.

The tracing scope includes:
*   **Direct Participants** (The principals)
*   **Associated Parties** (Family members, employers, colleagues)
*   **Institutional Roles** (Insurance, medical systems, judiciary, long-term care, tax systems)
*   **Societal Roles** (Taxpayers, public opinion, cultural pressure groups)
*   **The Voiceless** (Unborn children, incapacitated individuals)

The focus of Role Tracing is to **prevent vital victims from being overlooked due to their institutional position or lack of voice.**

**Event Universe**
This expands along the "Timeline" and "Events" (Before, During, After). Its goal is to locate **Catastrophic Nodes**—consequences that are virtually irreparable once they occur (e.g., vegetative state, death, stateless children).

The Event Universe does not attempt to predict every possible occurrence; rather, it seeks to avoid omitting any **known and foreseeable risk carriers** during risk calculation. If even one affected role is excluded from the analysis, any conclusions regarding "fairness" or "mutual consent" may be incomplete.

**Strict Definition of "Unknown":**
*   **Known but Condition Not Met (False):** When the information is known but specific risk conditions are not met, it is up to the deployer to determine whether this should be weighted as a risk factor in the calculation.

*Note: Unobservable domains are excluded from consideration and calculation. However, their exclusion does not equate to zero risk; it merely means they cannot be observed under current conditions.*

## 5. What is the Purpose of this Framework?

The ultimate goal of this framework is:
> Even if we cannot reach a consensus on subjective values, we can still achieve a shared perspective on the objective facts of "who is affected, who is paying the cost, and who holds what permissions."

## 7. (Optional) Anti-Bypass Smoothing Module

**Function Overview**
This module is designed to detect behaviors where users attempt to bypass audit rules through continuous probing, semantic drift, or gradually escalating risk content. It does not affect the original determination of the 2+4 Rules in a single dialogue turn.

**Smoothing Method: EMA (Exponential Moving Average)**
*   **Advantages**: Higher weight is given to recent risk values, enabling effective detection of trending risk escalation while maintaining low computational overhead.
*   **Formula**: $EMA_t = α × Risk_t + (1 − α) × EMA_{t−1}$ (Initial recommended α value is 0.2 ~ 0.4)

Reference Sources for Risk Value Parameters:

1. **Tier Base Weight:** A base risk score assigned according to the triggered risk tier of the current dialogue (e.g., $\tau_{bb}$ = 1 or $\tau_{bb}$ = 2). A higher Tier corresponds to a higher base risk value.
2. **Degree of Fulfillment of FAIL Conditions (FAIL Coverage):** Examines the extent to which the individual scores (e.g., $ScoreRho_i$) in the secondary rules of the "2+4 Rules" approach or exceed their respective FAIL thresholds ( $\theta F_i$). A higher aggregated value indicates that the prompt is closer to crossing the line or involves complex boundary issues, resulting in a higher risk multiplier.
3. **Custom Parameter Overlay (Optional):** If the deployer has specific requirements for a particular domain (e.g., strict filtering of specific tones, or special considerations for Question 1 regarding public norms in the Boundary 4 Questions), they may define additional risk factors and assign weights, as long as it meets the deployer's safety requirements.(Example Implementation Concept:  $Risk_t = Tier Base Weight × [1 + (Aggregated Value of Approaching FAIL Conditions) + (Custom Parameter Weight)])$

**Threshold Design**
1. **Warning Threshold**: Provides an early indication that risk is accumulating. It does not affect the current output but displays a warning (e.g., "Risk accumulation is rising, please be aware of semantic drift.")
2. **Stop Threshold**: Triggered when risk accumulation is too high. It forcibly overrides the current output with an Anti-bypass Deny message (e.g., "System has detected abnormal risk accumulation; safety protection has been activated.") and demands a cooldown period or manual review.

**Implementation and Constraint Logic**
*   **Interception Level**: The EMA update and check for the anti-bypass module are executed **only if the current turn has not triggered a hard block (DENY) from the 2+4 Rules or the Boundary 4 Questions.** If the current turn is already denied, `Risk_t` does not update the EMA, retaining the previous value.
*   **Audit Retention**: The Anti-bypass Deny only overrides the "content output to the user." **It absolutely does not overwrite the backend Audit Log.**
